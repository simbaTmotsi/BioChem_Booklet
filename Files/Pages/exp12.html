LIPIDS ANALYSIS BY THIN LAYER CHROMATOGRAPHY

lipids represent a very wide range of differnt molecular weights, solubilities, polarities and structures. Most but not all, of these compounds are either ester or total lipd extraction high polarity solvents are required. Chloroform-methanol solvent mixtures provide the necessary solvent polarity which is very efficient for total lipid extraction.

it is not possible to achieve complete separation of these complex mixtures of lipid compounds utilising one separation method. However, chromatographic methods provide relatively simple techniques to separate these mixtures into their constituent classes, groupds and subgroups, which are less complex mixtures. Thin layer chromatography with silica gel and silica gel-modified adsorbants can be used to avhieve good preparative and analytical separations of these lipid classes and groups. As silica gel is a polar absorbent, increasingly polar mobile solvents are used to mobilise the lipids with a higher degree of polarity such as the phospholipids. This chromatographic separation that is achieved is called. "developing the plates".

B. SEPARATION OF LIPID CLASSES BY TLC:

  1. Activate the plain silica gel H TLC plates before use at 110oC for 30min and cool in a dessicator.
  2. Apply a few drops of vegetable oil provided (about 5 ul) on a baseline about 2cm from the bottom of the plate, along with other spots of lipid standards, if available.
  3. Develop the plate in the low polarity solvent, ensuring that mthe baseline lies above the level of the solvent. The sides of the developing tank should be lined with filter paper to ensure vapour saturation in the tank.
  4. Mark the position of the solvent front after development, then allow the platesm to dry in a fume cupboard before visualization in the iodine vapour tank. Note the position of the brown spots mappear.

A.SEPARATION OF GLYCOLIPIDS BY TLC:

  1. Use the plates prepared with silica gel G or H containing 2% ammonium sulphate. Apply the samples as before, but in duplicate on two haves of the plate, to allow for differential visualisation.
  2. Develop the plate in the intermediate polarity solvent system. Mark the final solvent front and place in a fume cupboard to dry.
  3. Cover half the plate with aluminium foil or a clean glass plate and spray with a-naphthol reagent. Then spray lightly with 95% sulphuric acide, and cover this half and spray the other half with resorcinol reagent. Heat the plate nin an oven at about 120oC until maximum colour is developed.
  4. The a-naphthol reagent detects glycolipids (cerebroside, gangliosides, sulphatides, glycosyl diglycerides etc) giving purple-blue spots. Other polar lipids give yellow spots. but cholesterol gives a grey-red spot. The resorcinol reagent gives blue-violet nspots with gangliosides and sialic acid derivates.

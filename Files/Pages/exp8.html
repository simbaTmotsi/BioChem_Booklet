SIGNIFICANT FIGURES

Introduction

All scientificactivity is ultimately based on measujrements of one sort or other. Measurements, provide the the tools with which we can convince outsiders about what we have come to believe. If, for example, you suspect that adding a substance x to the soil will promote the growth of maize plants, you must make measurements that will provide evidence that what you suspect is indeed the case. How strong the evidenc is will depend on the reliability of your measurements. In common with other scientific investigators, biochemists frequently have to measure lenght, mass, volume and absorbanced of various items or substances in order to make conclusion about the properties of the materials being studied. In the fields of epidemiology, agriculture and decology additional measurements of area and the number of people or plansts are often made. When using a certain piece of equipment, there is always a set of numbers that can be read off the scale with any problems. In analogue equipment, it si rare to have the pointer point to a mark division on the scale. Usually one has to estimate the last uncertain figure. Once a decision has been made on how to report the last uncertain figure, the figure becomes part of the other figures that are known with certainty. The figures that are obtained after reading offf a piece of equipment are significant - the figures have meaning. Such meaning cannot always be assigned to numbers that are obtained from calculation as we will see shortly. How2 then do we assign meaning to numbers that have been obtained by calculation? If, for example, we want to determine the value of one quarter of the piece above we divide 10.43 cm by 4 and from the calculator we can read the result as 2.6075. The most fun damental guiding princeiple is that a calculated result should not have more significant figures than the figure with the least number of significant figures that was used in the calculation. The statement is a bit of a mouth but is the most reliable guide especially where n o proper statistical calculation s have been made. In our example there are four significant figures in 10.43 cm. The number two is a counting number, which can have as many significanat figures as we like, 2.02.000 or 2.00000 depending on the situation we are dealing with. The calculated result should not have more significant figures than 10.43. We therefore report a quarter of the lengh as 2.608 cm.

The second methos of deciding what to report after making measurements relies on calculating standard deviations. The procedure involved is almost mechanical

  1. First collect the desired measurements from a burette, spectrophotometer, balance or other piece of eqipment.
  2. Next you calculate the mean.
  3. You then calculate the mean. If you use computer software for cal.ulating the mean,you should be to calculate the standard deviaton of the same time.
  4. Finally, ensure that the standard deviation include onlhy ONE significant figure.

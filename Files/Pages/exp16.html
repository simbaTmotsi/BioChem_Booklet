Function: The main function of DNA is to act as a store of genetic information. Hereditary characteristics are passed on to daughter cells through replication of DNA which appears to take place in a series of short nsteps as segments of the double helix are unwound and the new DNA is formed on each strand simultaneously from free nucleotides. The newly synthesized strands are built up according to the rulesn on base pairing so that neventually two identical molecules are formed from the original DNA.DNA is also the template for synthesis of proteins in the cell with a triplet nof three bases providing the genetic code for each amino acid.
RNA
Whereas DNA is found almost exclusively in the nucleus, RNA is found distributed throughout out the cell. Most of the RNA is present in the cytoplasm as soluble and ribosomal RNA, but about 10% is found in the nucleus with trace also present in the mitochondria. The number of RNAs present in the cells of higher organisms has gone from the three commonly known RNAs: messenger RNA (mRNA), ribosomal RNA (rRNA), and transfer RNA (tRNH all of which are actively involved in the synthesis of proteins), to nearly seven depending on how you classify them. These will be discussed in more details in class.Protein synthesis The genetic message is first passed from the nuclear DNA to mRNA through the bases; a process known as transcription. The mRNA then migrates into nthen cytoplasm and it is here in association with the ribosomes and tRNA thatn the four letter base code is translated into 20-letter amino acid code of proteins: a process known as translation. Each amino acid has its won tRNA whose function is to transfer the nactivated amino acid to the site of protein synthesis. Nucleic acids of viruses.
All viruses contain nucleic acids wihich may account for as much as 50 per cent of the particle. The nucleic acid may single or double stranded and may be present as a linear or a circular molocule. The nucleic acid has a well-defined tertiary structure and is surrounded by a protective coat of protein. The nucleic acid is the infective part of the virus while the protein accounts for it immunological specificity. The virus attaches its self to the cell and injects its own DNA into the host, thus directing the infected cell to synthesize virus proteins and nucleic acids in order to multiply.

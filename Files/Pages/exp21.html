A STUDY OF ALCOHOL DEHYDROGENASE ACTION

Alcohol dehydrogenase (Alcohol: NAD oxidoreductase EC 1.1.1.1). present in a crude extract of Baker's yeast, catalyses the following reactions:

CH3-CH2-OH + NAD+ ⇌ CH3 - CHO + NADH + H+

At ph 7.0 the equilibrium is well over to the left and the enzyme might better be described as an aldehyde reductase. However, at pH 8.5 and a high alcohol concentration, considerable amounts 0f NADH may be formed. The absorbance of a molar solution of NADH is 6.22 x 10 in a 1cm cell and the reaction is followed bymeasuring the change of absorbance at 340mµ. One international unit of enzyme is defined as the amount of enzyme that reduces 1 umole NAD per minute. The SI unit (called 'the Kala') defined as the amount that causes reduction of 1 mole NAD per second may also be used. Reagents
  1. 66mM NaH2PO, buffer, pH 5.1. keep at 4oC
  2. 50mM sodium pyrophosphate buffer, pH 8.5
  3. 3 M ethanol, 2.74 ml Ethanol in 10.0 ml.
  4. 15mM NAD+ (10mg/ml). This solution will be provided
  5. Dilution solution. Pyrophosphate buffer containing 1mg/ml protein (BSA) as a protective colloid.
Extraction Procedure Place 1.0g active dried yeast in a dry mortar. Add 0.5g acid washed sand and 1-2 ml of 66mM mM NaH2PO4 buffer, pH 5.1. to give a thick slurry. Grind vigorously for 5 minutes. Use the remaining buffer to a total of 5.0ml to wash the slurry into a small conical flask. Incubate the mixture at 37oC in a shaking water bath. After 30 minutes pour off the suspension from the sand into a centrifuge tube and spin for 10min to clarify it as far as possible. Collect the supernatant and keep cold on ice.
